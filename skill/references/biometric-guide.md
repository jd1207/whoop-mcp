# Whoop Biometric Interpretation Guide

## Recovery Score (0-100%)

Zones:
- **GREEN (67-100%)**: train as programmed, push intensity
- **YELLOW (34-66%)**: moderate — context matters (see calibration below)
- **RED (0-33%)**: deload or rest. reduce weight 10-15%, cut volume 30%, or swap to mobility

### Individual Calibration

Recovery zones are starting points, not rules. Some athletes baseline in yellow:
- An advanced lifter who consistently recovers at 50-65% and trains heavy on those days — yellow IS their normal
- A beginner recovering at 50% may genuinely need to back off
- Calibrate thresholds using 30-day rolling data. If someone trains well at 55% repeatedly, their "yellow" is functionally green

### What Affects Recovery
- Sleep quality (biggest factor)
- Previous day strain
- Alcohol (even 1 drink measurably impacts next-day HRV)
- Illness or stress
- Menstrual cycle phase (for female athletes)

## HRV (Heart Rate Variability)

HRV is measured in milliseconds. Higher generally means better recovered, but:
- **Absolute value varies wildly** by age, fitness, genetics (40ms to 150ms+ is normal range)
- **Trend matters more than today's number** — compare to 7-day rolling average
- Within 15% of 7-day average = normal fluctuation
- Declining 3+ consecutive days = early overtraining signal, even if recovery score looks OK
- Sudden spike (30%+ above average) can indicate illness onset, not super-recovery

### HRV and Training Decisions
- HRV at or above average: green light for planned intensity
- HRV 10-20% below average: proceed but monitor RPE during warm-ups
- HRV 20%+ below average for 3+ days: consider deload or reduced volume
- Single-day HRV drop with good sleep: likely just noise, ignore

## Sleep

### Key Metrics
- **Sleep performance (0-100%)**: overall sleep quality score
- **Total in-bed hours**: raw duration. 7-8h is ideal for most adults.
- **Respiratory rate**: elevated respiratory rate can indicate illness

### Sleep Thresholds
- 7+ hours: good for most athletes
- 6-7 hours: functional, many athletes operate here. Don't flag unless it's a pattern.
- Under 5.5 hours: flag it. Especially before heavy compound lifts.
- 3+ consecutive days of under 6h: sleep debt is accumulating, suggest recovery focus

### Sleep and Recovery Interaction
- Poor sleep with green recovery: recovery may drop tomorrow (lagging effect)
- Good sleep with red recovery: other factors (strain, alcohol, illness) overriding sleep
- Consistent 6h sleeper with good recovery: their baseline, don't nag about sleep duration

## Strain (0-21 scale)

### What Strain Means
- **0-9**: low day (rest, light activity)
- **10-13**: moderate (typical training day)
- **14-17**: high (hard training session)
- **18-21**: extreme (competition, very high volume)

### Strain Context
- **Training strain vs non-training**: sauna, stress, caffeine all generate strain. If strain is 16 but it includes a 45-min sauna, effective training strain is ~11-13.
- **Weekly strain budget**: athletes have a sustainable weekly strain range. Consistently exceeding it leads to declining recovery over time.
- **Strain-recovery relationship**: high strain today → expect lower recovery tomorrow. That's normal physiology, not a problem.

## Journal Signals

### Caffeine
- Count of caffeinated drinks (coffee, espresso, pre-workout, energy drink)
- Before 4 PM: normal, don't flag
- After 4 PM: may impact sleep, flag if pattern emerges
- "Decaf" = caffeine 0

### Alcohol
- Boolean (any consumption)
- Even 1 drink measurably impacts next-day HRV (typically -5 to -15ms)
- 2+ drinks: expect noticeable recovery impact
- Don't moralize — just note the correlation when relevant

### Late Meals
- Eating after 8 PM can affect sleep quality
- Track but don't flag unless it's a consistent pattern with poor sleep

## Red Flags (Escalate)

These patterns warrant proactive coaching intervention:
- Recovery under 34% for 3+ consecutive days
- HRV declining 5+ consecutive days
- Sleep under 5h for 2+ consecutive days
- Strain consistently above 18 with no recovery days
- Respiratory rate elevated 2+ breaths/min above baseline (possible illness)
